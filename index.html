Learning Goal

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to add fractions with unlike denominators using equivalent fractions, and explain why a common denominator is necessary.

Standard: 4.NF.B.3 — Understand addition and subtraction of fractions as joining and separating parts referring to the same whole.

Warm-Up: The LCM Connection

Before students can add unlike fractions, they need a solid command of Least Common Multiples. This 5-minute warm-up is already pre-loaded in your lesson slides based on last week's exit ticket data — 8 students showed gaps with LCM.

Warm-up prompt: Find the LCM for each pair: (2, 4), (3, 4), (4, 6), (5, 10). Then discuss: why does the LCM matter when we're adding fractions?

Suggested facilitation: cold-call two students per problem, then transition with "Today you'll see exactly why LCM matters."

Key Concept: Why Denominators Must Match

Fractions represent parts of a whole. You can't combine parts unless they're the same size — the denominator tells you the size of each piece.

A common misconception (visible in your class data): students add both numerators and both denominators. For example: ½ + ⅓ = 2/5. This seems logical to students who think of fractions as two separate numbers, not as a single quantity.

Analogy that works: "Imagine adding 2 quarters and 3 dimes. You can't say you have 5 coins of the same value — you need a common unit first. Fractions are the same idea."

The four-step process:

  • Find the LCM of the denominators — this is your common denominator
  • Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with that denominator
  • Add the numerators, keeping the common denominator
  • Simplify the result if possible

Worked Example: Step by Step

Walk students through this example at the board before partner practice:

Problem: ½ + ⅓ = ?
Step 1: LCM(2, 3) = 6 → common denominator is 6
Step 2: ½ = 3/6  ·  ⅓ = 2/6
Step 3: 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6
Step 4: Can 5/6 be simplified? No — GCF(5,6) = 1.
Answer: 5/6 ✓

Now try together: ¾ + ⅙ = ? Give students 90 seconds, then reveal answer (11/12 → already simplified).

Try It: Partner Practice

Students work in pairs using the fraction strips handout. Pairs who finish early move to the challenge problems on the back.

Problem set (scaffold → challenge):
  • ½ + ¼ = ?
  • ⅓ + ⅙ = ?
  • ¾ + ⅛ = ?
  • ⅖ + 3/10 = ?
  • ★ Maya ran ⅔ mile before school and ¾ mile after. How far total?

Circulate and watch for the adding-denominators error. When you see it, ask: "How big is each piece? Are the pieces the same size yet?" — don't correct directly.

Exit Ticket

Collect exit tickets at the door. Results auto-score in Kiddom within 1 minute of student submission.

  1. ½ + ¼ = ?
  2. ⅔ + ⅙ = ?
  3. ★ A pizza is cut into thirds. Maria ate ⅓ and shared ⅙ with a friend. What fraction did she give away in total?

If more than 6 students miss problem 3, open with a real-world word problem warm-up at the start of tomorrow's lesson. Kiddom will flag this automatically in your Class Report.
